From Paper to Presence

When I was younger, I spent a lot of time sketching. What fascinated me wasn't accuracy ‚Äî it was the moment when a few lines could suggest depth, movement, or mood. A sketch never fully reproduces what you see, yet somehow it captures something real: intention, atmosphere, a feeling of being there.

I learned early on that representation is not just about translating 3D into 2D. It's about choosing what to preserve ‚Äî and what can be left unsaid.

That question stayed with me.

Learning to Work With Abstractions

I later found myself studying data science, working with another kind of abstraction: numbers, patterns, models. I learned how to describe human behavior through data ‚Äî how to compress complex realities into something measurable and communicable.

But something felt incomplete.
Data could explain what was happening, sometimes even why.
It rarely captured how it felt.

I kept wondering:
What happens to experience when we reduce it too much?
What gets lost ‚Äî and what might be preserved differently?

Encountering XR

During my undergraduate years, I took a course in interactive computing and began experimenting with digital interaction. Around that time, I first encountered XR through the NYU Reality Lab.

It wasn't perfect. The devices were limited. The visuals were sometimes rough. The illusion could break.

But despite those limitations, something important happened.

XR didn't try to describe space ‚Äî it placed you inside one.
It didn't ask you to imagine ‚Äî it let you move, look around, and orient yourself.

For the first time, I saw a medium that treated presence itself as a form of communication.

That shift mattered to me more than realism ever could.

What I'm Exploring Now

Today, I'm a graduate student in data science, still working with abstractions ‚Äî but I'm increasingly drawn to building experiences instead of explanations.

I'm interested in XR not as spectacle, and not as entertainment, but as a way to make experiences more accessible.

To let people learn by being there.
To understand through movement, space, and interaction.
To encounter ideas without needing them fully translated into words or numbers.

I see XR as a bridge between analysis and intuition ‚Äî between knowing about something and sensing it directly.

An exploration of how mouse-based input can reshape spatial interaction and the rules of play.

Why I Built This

I was curious about the relationship between control mechanics and spatial experience. In VR and 3D environments, we often think of interaction as either body-based or controller-based. But what happens when you use a simple 2D input like mouse movement to navigate a 3D space? How does that constraint shape what's possible?

[Image/Video/GIF of interaction]

What the Experience Is Like

The user does: Move the mouse to control a blade in 3D space, slicing through fruits that fall from above.

The system responds: Fruits react with physics, breaking apart when hit. The 3D camera perspective updates with mouse movement, creating a sense of spatial depth.

The perception: Despite using just a mouse, there's a strong sense of presence in 3D space‚Äî the constraint becomes the mechanic itself.

[Screenshot/GIF showing gameplay]

How It Works

Input tracking: Mouse position is mapped to both blade position and camera angle.

Spatial rendering: Three.js handles 3D rendering; fruit positions are calculated in world space.

Collision detection: Simple AABB (axis-aligned bounding box) checks determine when the blade intersects fruit.

Feedback: Visual breaking animations and score updates create immediate sensory response.

What I Learned

Constraint breeds creativity: The limitation of mouse-based input forced interesting design choices. It wasn't about replicating VR‚Äîit was about finding what mouse input could do uniquely.

Spatial presence isn't about fidelity: Players felt genuinely present in the space even though the graphics were simple and the input method was 2D.

Next steps: I'd like to explore hybrid input‚Äîcombining mouse with other modalities (like voice or gesture) to see how that changes the spatial experience further.

An exploration of how facial recognition and speech transform the way we communicate across distance.

Why I Built This

I was thinking about translation‚Äînot just language, but the way emotions and intentions get transformed as they travel from one person to another. What if we could make those transformations visible? What if emotion itself became the data being passed through the system?

[Image/Video of interaction]

What the Experience Is Like

The user does: Person A speaks and shows their face to a camera. Their emotion (detected via facial recognition) and their words (captured via speech-to-text) become input.

The system responds: The message gets transformed through three canvases: facial data becomes visual patterns, words get broken and reassembled, the result is displayed as abstract representations rather than literal text.

The perception: The viewer sees the original message, then sees how it changes. The loss and transformation becomes the art itself.

[Screenshot showing the three canvases]

How It Works

Facial recognition: A pre-trained model detects emotion (happy, sad, surprised, etc.) and maps it to visual parameters.

Speech-to-text: Real-time transcription captures what's being said.

Three-canvas transformation: Each canvas applies different algorithmic transformations (glitch effects, color shifts, word scrambling) to show progressive "degradation" of the message.

What I Learned

Emotion as data is complex: Facial recognition can detect surface emotions, but it misses nuance. The system sometimes got it wrong‚Äîand those failures were actually more interesting.

Abstraction can communicate better than literal representation: By refusing to show the exact words or emotions, the piece invited interpretation rather than consumption.

Next steps: I wanted to make this physical. What if the transformation wasn't just visual? This eventually led to Worry Blower.

An exploration of bringing digital emotion-sensing into physical space through breath and light.

Why I Built This

After building Unravel the Message, I wondered: what if the system didn't just show emotion, but responded to it with physical presence? What if people could interact with something that literally breathes with them?

What the Experience Is Like

The user does: Stand in front of the installation. Your face is recognized, your voice is heard, your worries are captured.

The system responds: A mechanical fan controlled by Arduino responds to your emotional state. LED lights shift color based on detected emotion. The installation literally breathes‚Äî exhaling and inhaling in sync with your emotional energy.

The perception: You're not just interacting with a screen. The system's presence in the room makes the interaction feel reciprocal‚Äî it's not just reading you; it's responding with its own physical presence.

[Video of breathing effect]

How It Works

Facial recognition + speech-to-text: Same core as Unravel; detects emotion and captures words.

Arduino connection: p5.js communicates with Arduino via serial to control motor speed and LED colors.

Gear mechanism: Mechanical gears translate motor rotation into fan blade movement, creating that visceral sense of "breathing."

What I Learned

Physical presence changes everything: The same emotion-sensing system felt fundamentally different when it had a body. People took it more seriously. They felt more heard.

Technical constraints taught design lessons: The fan had limited speed range, so we had to be creative about how emotion mapped to breath rate.

Next steps: Explore multi-sensory feedback‚Äîwhat if the installation could also produce sound or heat? How would that deepen the sense of presence further?

Why I Built This

To build immersive experiences, I needed to learn how to think spatially. These models are my alphabet‚Äîeach one taught me something about form, proportion, and how objects exist in space.

[Carousel or grid of all models]

The Models

Headphones: Learning curves and hard-surface modeling.

Teddy Bear: Organic forms and soft shapes.

Toast: Simplicity and how to convey texture.

Shin-chan: Character proportions and expressive form.

Desk & Chair: Spatial relationships and how objects interact.

What I Learned

Form teaches itself: By modeling different objects, I developed intuition about how shapes work together and what makes something feel "right" spatially.

Constraints shape creativity: Each modeling challenge required different technical approaches, which taught me flexibility.

Next steps: Integrate these models into immersive experiences. Spatial fluency is foundational; now I want to use it to build worlds.
